Strategy in a month-long prisoner's dilemma¶

Overview¶

What strategies do players develop when they play the 10-round prisoner's dilemma game 20 times a day over the course of a month? First, let us imagine if you were one of the players. In each round, you and one other opponent opp can choose to cooperate C or defect D to receive the following payoffs:

if opp chooses C if opp chooses D
if you choose C you receive R you receive S
if you choose D you receive T you receive P

You should also pay attention to what your opponent receives:

if opp chooses C if opp chooses D
if you choose C opp receives R opp receives T
if you choose D opp receives S opp receives P

You might wonder, what are R, S, T, and P? The paper we take our data from - Mao et al. 2017 - uses the following RSTP values:

if opp chooses C if opp chooses D
if you choose C you receive 5 points you receive 1 points
if you choose D you receive 7 points you receive 3 points

Points might seem abstract, but the stakes involve real money: for every 2 points a player receives in the game, he earns 1 cent afterwards. Notice that:

  • There is a temptation T to defect while your opponent cooperates, as this gives a larger payoff for you than the reward R for both players cooperating.
  • But the reward R for both players cooperating is larger than the punishment P for both players defecting, so there is also a motivation to cooperate.
  • But cooperating while your opponent defects gives the least payoff overall, so there is a motivation to avoid being suckered S.
  • In addition, in terms of balance, the reward R is larger than the average of being suckered S and tempted T.

Altogether, this looks like:

$$T>R>P>S$$

and $$R>\frac{S+T}{2}$$

Strategies¶

So what are the strategies that players develop to maximize their payoff? We can get some idea of what to expect by looking at Axelrod's tournaments. In these tournaments, Axelrod invited game theorists to submit strategies to compete against one another. In the end, the successful strategies tried to:

  • Be nice: Cooperate first and do not defect before your opponent.

  • Retaliate: Do not continue to cooperate if your opponent defects.

  • Forgive: Be open to cooperating again if your opponent does not continue to defect.

  • Do not be greedy: Do not try to score more than your immediate opponent, only try to maximize your own score over the whole tournament.

In the games we are about to look at, the players will not be familiar with the success of these strategies. And unlike pre-programmed strategies, they could become emotional and make different choices than above. An aggresive player, for example, might reason as follows:

  • Be nice? No! I can cooperate at first, but I will also defect first to avoid being suckered.

  • Retaliate? Sure! I should not let my opponent walk all over me.

  • Forgive? No! I should continue to retaliate, my opponent has betrayed my trust.

  • Do not be greedy? No! I should always try to get the better of my opponent.

With this in mind, let us look at the games and see the strategies of successful players on the last day of play (Day 20). There are about 90 players playing 20 ten-round games over the course of the day.

  • Remove incomplete games, namely games that do not have 20 actions (2 players acting over the course of 10 rounds)
  • Divide the large dataset into the individual days

Looks like for each game, many of the more successful players often cooperate with their opponent for 8 rounds R, defect while the opponent continues to cooperate T, defect again while the opponent moves to defect P, and avoid being suckered S. The top-scoring players seem to alternate this strategy with cooperating for 9 rounds and defecting on round 10.

Although we are not yet looking at how players play round-by-round, at this point we can make hypothesis for how the most successful players strategize (Axelrod identified strategies are in blue and any violations are in red):

  • (Round 1-8): Start off the round by cooperating ('Be nice')
  • (Round 9): Defect while the opponent cooperates ('Not being nice')
  • (Round 10): Defect while the opponent defects ('Retaliate')

But what about 'Forgive'? Here Axelrod's ideas might be less applicable. In Axelrod's tournaments, each strategy plays against another strategy for 200 rounds each game. In the games we are looking at, each player plays against another player for 10 rounds each game. This does not seem to be enough time to 'Forgive' since most defections start near the end of each game. Also, being consistently suckered even just once a game is enough to drop you to the bottom of our list while defecting before the opponent defects every game puts you at the top of our list. There just is not the same flexibility to 'Forgive' as in Axelrod's tournaments, where being suckered a couple times at the end of 200 rounds has much less weight than being suckered at the end of 10 rounds.

But what about 'Don't be greedy'? If the sole focus is to never score worse than your current opponent, the strategy would be to defect immediately: you will never do worse than your opponent and you will never be suckered, but your scores will always be the lowest at the end of the day. The highest-scoring strategy is opening to cooperating and thus open to scoring lower than the immediate opponent, but minimizes the risk of being suckered by correctly assuming most opponents will not defect earlier than it anticipates.
