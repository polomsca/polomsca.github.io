Cycles of hype and disappointment are common in emergent technologies and the
development of artificial intelligence is no exception. In the case of artificial intelligence, this
hype is understandable: since antiquity, machines capable of human thought have appeared in
the fiction of nearly every civilization. It stands to reason that the pace of artificial intelligence
development quickened once the analogy was drawn between observations of all-or-nothing
electrical signals in the neural networks of the brain and the development of electronic
computers that could perform any form of computation digitally. If the 40s and 50s saw the
emergence of artificial intelligence as its own distinct field, the years that followed marked a
period of rapid progress accompanied by a meteoric rise in optimism, the expectation being an
intelligent machine would be built within twenty years.
1
Hindsight gives an opportunity to laugh at such outrageous claims, but hindsight also
allows an opportunity to observe the language proponents of artificial intelligence have used to
define their field and how this has influenced the course of funding for their endeavors. The twin
issues of definition and funding find their root at the very term artificial intelligence. Namely the
term artificial intelligence was itself an attempt to avoid associations with cybernetics, which
could have well been seen as a natural precursor to artificial intelligence. Official histories often
cite the second Dartmouth Conference of 1956 as the formal beginnings of the field of AI, but for
the purposes of this narrative, it also serves as a natural starting point as when the term
artificial intelligence first came into widespread use. Yet how artificial intelligence was chosen
to be defined is somewhat marred by less-than-scientific rhetoric. As when prompted, the
organizer of the conference remarked:

Rockefeller Foundation, which had previously funded research that allowed Wieners

doing so, earned its first paycheck cut.

Thus the Minsky and Papert text is often cited as having killed the perceptron, and
indeed, in the same year their book was published, mainstream funding for perceptron, and by
extension, most other connectionist projects, evaporated as support shifted to symbolic AI.
Connectionist projects, though they remained afloat, were no longer seen as legitimate
approaches to artificial intelligence. Once again, the funding and the re-definition of artificial
intelligence remain irrevocably intertwined. It took nearly ten years for the connectionist
approach to reemerge, and its recent domination in the realm of classification have led to heavy

Again, the language is not important, but for reference, in modern multilayer perceptrons, neurons are
organized in layers, an input layer, multiple hidden layers, and an output layer. For example, lets say you
are training a multilayer perceptron to recognize handwritten numbers 0 to 9 from the MNIST dataset. If

inputs (28x28) and the output layer should have 10 neurons pumping out estimates (for the digits 0 to 9).
In between are a number of hidden layers each with a number of neurons. Each neuron in each layer
should be connected to every neuron in the previous layer. So say if there was one hidden layer of 500
neurons, then there would be 784x500 total connections between the input layer and the hidden layer,
and 500x10 total connections between the hidden layer and the output layer. This is standard for the
simplest MLPs today, however, at the time of Minsky and Paperts writing, it was popular to use MLPs

Recent progress (or rather, recent re-emergence of various methods) in artificial
intelligence within even the last five years have seen unprecedented breakthroughs in voice
recognition and pattern recognition, but also comes at the heels of AI winters as recent as the
90s. Once again the field of artificial intelligence is undergoing a redefinition. The various
methods which began in artificial intelligence and have been declared successes, often fall out
of being defined explicitly as artificial intelligence to populate fields as machine learning and
more broadly, data science. Part of this is doubtless the AI effect: true artificial intelligence has
so long eluded human attempts that artificial intelligence has become defined simply as
anything still out of reach of current methods in artificial intelligence. Part of this may come from
the negative associations with the term artificial intelligence, as branded into the public by the
numerous AI winters.
Nevertheless, modern hype for artificial intelligence (explicitly) is building. Google and
Facebook have recently begun numerous acquisitions in the field of AI, which have prompted a
surge of AI startups whose sole purpose is suspected of becoming acqui-hired by technology
