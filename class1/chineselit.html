Elements of fantasy or imagination often serve to underscore exemplary virtue or purity
that otherwise would not be addressed through the normal course of a realistic narrative. Across
genres, an abrupt introduction of a fantastical element offers new avenues in storytelling,
oftentimes to twist a tragic fate into something more akin to poetic justice. For instance, both
Snow in Midsummer, a Yuan variety play, and An Actress Scorns Wealth and Honor to
Preserve Her Chastity, a Qing vernacular story, exemplify two instances of the supernatural,
the first at the death - or apparent death - of the lead character, and the second at the reunion of
the lead with their family as a ghost - or seemingly as a ghost. The first instance demonstrates
purity of resolve in light of injustice, and the second instance sets into motion the events that
eventually correct a previous injustice. And while these deus ex machina instances may indeed
be a result of sloppy authors writing themselves into corners, attributing authorial intent to
placing supernatural events in otherwise realistic stories may allow further commentary on what
constitutes as virtue in a society of practicalities.
For instance, in Snow in Midsummer, Guan Han Qing first develops a premise that
appears to be realistic at its outset. The play follows Dou E, a young girl whose father was
forced to marry her off to pay his debts. However, her husband dies soon after marriage and her
mother-in-law, Mrs. Cai, is also widowed. Because of this, they become harassed by Zhang Lu
Er, who, after saving Mrs. Cais life, feels entitled to ask for Dou Es hand in marriage. When
Zhang Lu Er tries to force Dou Es hand by falsely accusing her of poisoning his father, she
continues to defy him until the court threatens to beat her mother-in-law, at which point she
takes responsibility for the crime and is wrongly executed. All events leading up the the
execution, improbable though some may be, are still firmly set in reality. With such a premise, it
seems inevitable that the play will end in tragedy, with the innocent Dou E killed for protecting
her mother-in-law while the perpetrator Zhang Lu Er walks free because of a negligent prefect.
Even Dou Es lines before execution seem to preclude a fantastical deus ex machina, as
she curses both heaven and earth for not saving her. For:
The good are poor, and die before their time;
The wicked are rich, and live to a great age.
The gods are afraid of the mighty and bully the weak;
They let evil take its course. (37)
These lines are in lyric, which is significant as lines that are sung in a variety play often can
provide deeper meta-commentary about the play itself. In Snow in Midsummer, the singing role
is assumed by Dou E, and if she claims that They [the gods] let evil take its course, the
audience can hold a reasonable assumption that the play with end in tragedy with rampant
injustice (37).
However, just before Dou E is to be killed, she makes three impossible claims. First, she
claims that after she is beheaded, not a drop of her blood will spill onto the ground. Second,
three feet of snow will fall even though it is midsummer. And third, the district will suffer drought
for the next three years. Dou E makes these claims, swearing that if she was wrongly executed,
then these three phenomena will come to pass. And indeed, even before the executioner
manages to swing his blade, snow begins to fall and Dou E manages to croak out Now snow
will show the injustice done to me! before she is finally beheaded (40). This turn of events is
surely fantastical. Each claim is supernatural at best, and the timing of the snow to coincide with
Dou Es declaration creates a sense that Heaven itself is responding to her wishes. If the play
stuck with its realistic premise, it would find its end after the execution and the moral would align
closer to Dou Es original laments that heaven cannot discriminate evil. Thus, the first fantastical
element introduced in the penultimate act allows Guan Han Qing to let the narrative to explore
the idea of resolving injustice in the final act.
The second fantastical element involves Dou Es ghost in the final act. Her father, now a
government official, reviews the old cases, but does not understand the significance of Dou Es
docket and puts it out of mind. Seeing this, Dou Es ghost appears to her father in a dream and
discloses the true state of affairs. Now convinced of the wrong-doing, Dou Es father has Zhang
Lu Er summarily executed, and the prefect put out of office. Thus, Dou Es ghostly reunion with
her father ultimately provides the stimulus to right previous wrongs. However, it is possible that
this story could have been told without a fantasy element. One can imagine that Dou E could
have been imprisoned instead of executed, and that the conversation with her father could
happen in a jail cell instead of in a dream. But Guan Han Qings introduction of the fantastical
element allows an exploration of the narrative after tragedy has irreversibly left its mark. That is,
if read as a political allegory, Dou Es death can represent the death of the native Han dynasties
by the foreign Yuan dynasty. That in itself is not too far-fetched: Dou Es death are a result of
the machinations of Zhang Lu Er, whose name can be a homophonic pun on donkey, which
can be seen as a derogatory assignment. Furthermore, Zhang Lu Er plans on marrying Dou E
to inherit her mother-in-laws vast estate, provoking her lament that He [Mrs. Cais late
husband] tilled the land, but others [Zhang Lu Er] are reaping the harvest, drawing not so subtle
parallels to the Mongol conquest of Chinese territory (28).
By having Dou E die, Guan Han Qing can lament the finality of the death of the Chinese
dynasties and commemorate defiance against injustice. Yet by having Heaven answer Dou Es
cries for snowfall, and allowing Dou Es ghost to speak to her father, Guan Han Qing can push
for the interpretation that even though the Chinese people as represented in Dou E believe
heaven and earth have since forsaken them, their indomitable virtue does not go unnoticed.
Perhaps the poetic justice established at plays end is still wishful thinking on Guan Han Qings
part, but the sentiment is communicated nonetheless.
A similar narrative structure is also found in An Actress Scorns Wealth and Honor to
Preserve Her Chastity, even though it is a Qing vernacular story by the Li Yu, a writer
determined to showcase his own originality through discarding the influence of previous
literature. Again, the premise is explored realistically at its outset. Liu Miaogu catches the eye of
a young orphaned scholar Tan Chuyu, who immediately joins her mothers acting troupe to get
closer to Miaogu under the pretense of his interest in drama. However, by the decorum of the
acting troupe, developing a relationship between actors and actresses was forbidden, and no
expression of love was permitted between the two. Thus, Liu Miaogu and Tan Chuyu could only
express their love onstage as characters in a play, and kept their vows secret offstage. This
comes to a front when Liu Miaogus mother pushes to marry her off to a rich patron. After a
failure to negotiate, Liu Miaogu performs in one final play, and when the play calls for her
suicide, she acts it out in reality, jumping into the river, with Tan Chuyu a step behind. Were this
action a true double-suicide, the storys themes might focus on Liu Miaogus mother, who saw
her daughter only as a money-tree even after her death, or the central focus might be on the
inversion of Liu Miaogu and Tan Chuyu acting out their true emotions in theatre, while hiding
their affections in real life. Ending in tragedy, the story would only address the pity of forcing a
young couple to despair.
However, as a storyteller, Li Yu intervenes. Although there are indeed many peculiarities
with his story as is, no one instance required much suspension of disbelief. However, the first
imaginative element breaks this immersion (and one wonders whether Li Yu is determined to
spend the remainder of the story amidst in wishful thinking). For Liu Miaogu and Tan Chuyu do
not, in fact, die. On the contrary, it seemed as if someone amidst the waves were leading them
[Tan and Miaogu] together and preventing them from drifting apart. It seemed, too, as if a
gigantic fish were carrying them on its back along the surface of the water, so that they didnt
drown in the course of traveling a hundred miles, (Owen 932). The miraculous fish then
seemed to deliver Tan and Miaogu to their fishermen rescuers and leave contently in fulfillment
of its role. Even Tan and Miaogu knew that they owed their lives to Lord Yans miraculous
powers, (Owen 933). Blessings from the theatre god, much like the snowfall in Snow in
Midsummer, seem to be implicit approval of the determination of the lead characters, Dou E for
going to her death in face of injustice, and Tan and Miaogu, for attempting to leap to their
deaths in face of uncompromising circumstances.
Yet the story does not end there. After their near-death experiences, Tan and Miaogu
turn their fortunes around and Tan in particular manages to pass his exams to become a judge.
But a second fantastical element encroaches. Ever thankful for the Lord Yans blessing, Tan
and Miaogu make their way to his temple to pay respects. However, they fail to reach the
temple in time for the birthday of Lord Yan. But the ritual play in celebration of Lord Yan was
delayed due to unnatural rain, and so Tan and Miaogu arrive in time for the troupe to begin their
performance. Coincidence though this was, remarks the ever-present narrator, the power of
the god may also have had something to do with it. Perhaps he wanted this romance, which
began in the theatre, to end in the theatre, and so delayed the festival until their arrival, thus
setting up a Happy Reunion finale (Owen 936). Of course, with the fourth wall in a perilous
condition of falling completely over, the troupe designated to perform at the temple is none other
than Tan and Miaogus original acting troupe.
Naturally, in preparation for some poetic justice, Tan makes a donation and requests the
troupe perform the same play he and Miaogu last performed before their attempted double-
suicide. And so the second fantastical element - the heavy rainfall and suspicious coincidence
of meeting the old acting troupe - has set into motion the change of heart. Before, it was
Miaogus uncompromising mother that forced Miaogus hand, as she saw her daughter only as
a source of money to be made. Now, when she performed the same play she believed her
daughter died in, she could control herself no longer and burst into unrestrained sobbing.
Whereas before she had cried out Qian Yulian, wife, where are you?, she now, as she
continued sobbing, forgot where she was and substituted the word child for wife. The
audience knew she was weeping for Miaogu, but not even Master Zhou would have had the
heart to criticize her mistake, (Owen 938).
Seeing her change of heart, Miaogu cannot help but reveal herself. Initially the reunion is
troubled: much like the meeting between Dou E and her father in Snow in Midsummer, the
meeting between Miaogu and her mother begins on the wrong foot, with her mother, the troupe,
and the entire audience believing they are witnessing Miaogus ghost. However, unlike Snow in
Midsummer, Tan and Miaogu are still very much alive, and with that resolved, the mother-
daughter relationship is repaired, with the mother valuing her daughter more than what wealth
she can bring through marriage. But again, as seen in Snow in Midsummer, the fantastical
elements seem not strictly necessary within the narrative proper. Presumably Tan and Miaogu
could have escaped the troupe by more conventional means, and perhaps they could have
consciously tracked down their old acting troupe if they still thought a reunion would be fitting.
Yet the the imaginative elements of the otherwise realistic story adds a transformative aspect,
and the transformation is so dramatic it might as well be that Tan and Miaogu have died in some
regard. For before their leap into the river, Tan and Miaogu were constantly preoccupied with a
loss of agency. They were trapped and could never express themselves within the bounds of
the acting troupe. After their rescue, Tan and Miaogu had unprecedented freedom and each
one of their choices were of their own free will.
An additional transformation concerns the role of wealth in the story. In the first half of
the story, Tan and Miaogus fates were irrevocably tied to the idea of wealth. Miaogus mother
attempted to marry her off for the express purpose of having her never want for anything the
rest of [her] life, (Owen 926). Of course, in saying that, Miaogus mother has already accepted
the betrothal gift, showing her utter disregard for anything save money. Yet in the second half of
the story, Tan and Miaogu have the means to enjoy wealth and prestige from Tans government
office, but ultimately reject excessive fortune as well. Following Fisherman Mos example, who
saved their lives after the fish deposited them unceremoniously, and who refused Tans insistent
offers to repay him excessively, Tan and Miaogu abandoned the idea to seek promotion in the
capital or enjoy [Tans] hometown glory, (Owen 940). Instead, Tan brought an acre or two of
hilly ground and built a thatched hut on it. He spent his days fishing, partly to follow the loft
example of Yan Ziling and partly to receive Fisherman Mos guidance, (Owen 940). The
fantastical brought about the impetus for such a dramatic change, which, in the blessing of Tan
and Miaogu, gave them a second life of fortune, and later, allowed Tan to come to terms with
abandoning his idea of luxury to live as a fisherman.
While Guan Han Qing seems to champion the indomitable spirit of the upright with his
addition of the fantastical in Snow in Midsummer, Li Yus motivations are not as loud. Perhaps
with the transformation provided by the river and the heavy rain, Li Yu is able to free his
characters from the ugly pursuit of wealth and allow them to engage in a simple lifestyle almost
reminiscent of Tao Yuan Mings pastoral ideals (certainly the thatched hut and fishermans
lifestyle evoke common images). Tan and Miaogus free pursuit of the simple also draws parallel
with the Tao Yuan Mings Peach Blossom Spring, where immortals live their lives in a Daoist
utopia. In light of this, Li Yus throwaway comment that Tan and Miaogu lived on into their
nineties seems to channel this particular line of thought (Owen 940).
Thus, in both Snow in Midsummer and An Actress Scorns Wealth and Honor to
Preserve Her Chastity, a fantastical, often transformative, element underlines the virtue of the
lead characters against the injustices beset upon them and sets the stage for a second
fantastical reunion between the leads and the families, leading to a correction of previous
injustice. While this narrative structure is by no means universal, it finds common purpose in
these dissimilar works, and allows the audience to acknowledge the severity of the tragic
elements of the play, as they could only be overturned by miraculous and fantastical means. In
turn, the reunion and reversal of the previous tragedies allows the authors true ideals to shine,
though often this pushes the previously realistic work into what is clearly imaginative territory.
